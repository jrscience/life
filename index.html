Taxonomy

Taxonomy is a fancy word for stamp collecting. It's how scientists try to categorise all the different weird and wonderful types of life on Earth.

Scientists have had many attempts over the years and come up with different systems. It is not so straightforward due to the sheer diversity of life on Earth.

The modern system uses eight distinct catgeories - as shown below.

DOMAIN --> KINGDOM --> PHYLUM --> CLASS --> ORDER --> FAMILY --> GENUS --> SPECIES

Each category gets more specific (fewer organisms) as you go from left to right.

One way to remember these is using a mnemonic, e.g. Dear King Phillip Came Over For Good Soup

Humans are classified as follows:
Domain: Eukaryota
Kingdom: Animalia
Phylum: Chordata
Class: Mammalia
Order: Primates
Family: Hominidae
Genus: Homo
Species: Homo sapiens

The field of taxonomy can get quite involved, but we can try to understand things simply.

  • Three domains: Archaea, Bacteria, Eukarya
    • Archae and Bacteria are microorganisms. They are single-celled. Eukarya include plants, animals and fungi - typically more complex forms of life (though single-celled eukaryotes exist.) Eukaryotes have cells that contain a nucleus as well as other organelles.
  • Seven kingdoms (Note: the number of kingdoms and precise classifications vary from source to source): Archaea, Bacteria, Protozoa, Chromista, Plantae, Fungi, Animalia
    • Being very simplistic (perhaps even a little crude) about things, we could think of the kingdoms above as something like: Bacteria, Plantae, Fungi, Animalia, Everything else.
    • Archaea, Bacteria, Protozoa and Chromista are simple forms of life (mostly single-celled.) Plantae are plants, Fungi are fungi (the plural of fungus) and Animalia are animals.
  • Examples of phyla (plural of phylum) within the Animal kingdom include: Porifera (sponges), Cnidaria (marine animals such as coral and jellyfish), Platyhelminthes and Annelida (worms), Molluscs (slugs and snails, but also many marine creatures such as clams and mussels), Anthropods (insects and crustaceans) and Chordata (most animals we're familiar with - dogs, horses, birds, and humans!)
  • Examples of classes within the Animal kingdom include: Mammalia (mammals, e.g. humans and most zoo animals), Aves (birds), Amphibia (amphibians, e.g. frogs and toads), Reptilia (reptiles, e.g. lizards), Insecta (insects), Arachnida (arachnids, e.g. spiders and scorpions)
  • Examples of orders within the Animal kingdom include: Rodentia (rodents, e.g. mice, rats and squirrels), Chiropptera (bats), Cetacea (porpoises and whales) and Primates (gorillas, chimpanzees, humans)
  • Families group together animals with very similar features. As an example, the order Carnivora breaks down into Felidae (cats), Canidae (dogs), Ursidae (bears) and Mustelidae (weasels)
  • Genus is even more specific. Animals in the same genus are closely related. As an example, the cat family (Felidae) breaks down into Felis (small and domestic cats), Panthera (tigers, leopards, jaguars and lions) and Puma (panthers and cougars)
  • Finally, we get to species which is the most specific category. E.g. all humans are Homo sapiens (Homo is the genus, Homo sapiens is the species.)
  • Defining species precisely is also tricky business. Below is the definition according to Britannica (https://www.britannica.com/science/species-taxon)

    species, in biology, classification comprising related organisms that share common characteristics and are capable of interbreeding.

  • While below is the Cambridge Dictionary definition (https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/species)

    a set of animals or plants in which the members have similar characteristics to each other and can breed with each other

    • Note with regard to the above: it is rather animal focussed. Examples given are not exhaustive. Credit must be given to the website https://a-z-animals.com/reference/animal-classification/ from which I have taken many of the aforementioned examples.
